    Culture


    New Zealand is home to many different cultures, traditions and beliefs.
    The Maori culture is an integral part of New Zealand's history and identity.
    The indigenous Maori people arrived in New Zealand over a thousand years ago and have since played
    a significant role in shaping the country's culture and traditions. Maori culture is based on a deep respect
    for nature and the environment, with traditional practices and beliefs centered on the land and sea.
    The Maori language, or Te Reo, is still spoken today and is an important part of everyday life and culture in New Zealand.
    One of the most iconic symbols of Maori culture is the haka, a traditional war dance that is now performed
    on a variety of occasions to express strength, unity, and cultural identity, most famously seen worldwide performed by
    NZ rugby team the All Blacks. Other important aspects of Maori culture include carving, weaving, and tattooing,
    each of which has a rich history and complex symbolism.

    Today, the Maori culture continues to thrive in New Zealand, with Maori language and culture classes offered in our schools,
    Maori art and artifacts displayed in museums, and Maori cultural events and festivals celebrated throughout the country, such as Matariki the Māori New Year .
